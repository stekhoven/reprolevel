# Level 3 – Sweet Spot

*Robust and repeatable – the comfortable balance of rigour and effort.*

## What does Level 3 look like?

At **Level 3**, you’ve hit the **“sweet spot”** of reproducible research. This is the level where **good practices become routine** and your workflow is both *reasonably robust* and *reasonably easy to maintain*. You’ve moved past the precarious ad-hoc days (Level 1) and the just-in-time notes of Level 2, and now your analyses are structured and largely automated. Importantly, you’ve adopted key tools and habits from the *“research software engineering”* world, but without going so far that maintaining your workflow becomes a full-time job.

In practical terms, **Level 3 means you can re-run your entire analysis (or major parts of it) with confidence on your own machine, and your close colleagues likely could as well** with minimal help. You have a systematic way to execute your code, you’re using version control properly, and you’re capturing your computing environment well enough that others can recreate it. There might still be some manual steps and the process may not be bulletproof on a completely new computer, but for your team’s purposes, it’s usually “reproducible enough”. Many researchers find that this is the most efficient, sustainable spot to aim for in typical projects – hence, the *sweet spot*.

If Level 1 was cooking without a recipe, and Level 2 was hurriedly scribbling a recipe after cooking, **Level 3 is like a well-tested recipe written clearly**. You have a list of ingredients (software and versions) and step-by-step instructions (scripts) that you follow. A colleague with a similar kitchen (computing setup) should be able to cook the same dish successfully, though a chef in a different country might need to tweak a few things to account for local ingredients or oven temperatures.

## Typical characteristics and behaviours

*   **Version-controlled codebase:** You’re actively using Git (or another VCS) to manage your code. This isn’t just a backup; you write meaningful commit messages, organise your repository logically, and perhaps use branches for different features or explorations. Your project likely lives on a platform like GitHub or GitLab, which makes it easier to share with collaborators. The days of files named `analysis_final_REALLY_v3.R` are behind you, replaced by commits and tags.
*   **Structured, script-based workflow:** Your analysis is carried out through scripts or a simple pipeline. Instead of a haphazard collection of commands, you might have an **organised sequence of scripts** (like `01_data_prep.py` -> `02_analysis.py` -> `03_plot_results.py`) or a master script that runs everything in order. Alternatively, you might use a Makefile or a lightweight workflow tool (for example, an R **drake** plan or a simple Snakemake with a few rules) to chain steps together. The upshot: there’s a clear path from raw data to final results that doesn’t rely on you pressing buttons in the right order – the order is encoded in code.
*   **Explicit environment capture:** By now you’ve felt the pain of “it worked on my machine” and taken steps to prevent it. You’ve listed your project’s dependencies, perhaps in a `requirements.txt` (for Python) or an `environment.yml` for Conda, or you use R’s **renv** to lock package versions. You might not yet be containerising everything (that comes in Level 4), but you ensure that you (and others) know exactly which packages and software are needed to run the analysis. Re-running the project on a new machine starts with installing the listed dependencies.
*   **Improved documentation:** Your documentation has matured. The README is more detailed, possibly covering installation and usage of the code. You might have a wiki or a `docs/` folder for more extensive documentation, or at least well-commented code and clear commit history that tells the story of the project. Each script likely starts with a description of what it does, and any tricky steps or manual interventions are clearly noted so they’re not forgotten.
*   **Regularised data management:** Data files are handled with more care. You might use consistent filenames and possibly store raw data separately from processed data. Maybe you’ve started using a tool for dataset versioning (like Git LFS or DVC) if your data is large, or you’re at least keeping old copies of datasets with version labels. Data cleaning and processing steps are scripted, so original raw data is never overwritten.
*   **Collaboration-ready processes:** Because of version control and documentation, multiple people can work on the project in parallel. You might be using branches and pull requests, which introduces basic code review: colleagues comment on each other’s code changes, improving quality. There’s a shared understanding in the team of the project structure and guidelines (possibly unwritten, or documented in the README). New team members can get started by reading the docs and pulling the repository, rather than sitting through lengthy one-on-one debriefs.
*   **Maybe some continuous integration:** Some Level 3 projects experiment with CI for convenience. For example, you could have set up a GitHub Action to run a script to ensure it produces outputs, or to deploy the latest version of your documentation. But this is not a universally present feature at Level 3 – it’s more of a bonus for the enthusiasts. Automated testing is still minimal or ad hoc. The norm is to run things manually as needed, but with relative ease because of the structure you’ve put in place.

## Typical tools and artefacts

*   **Git repository with a clear structure:** Expect to see a repository containing folders like `data/`, `src/` or `scripts/`, `results/` or `figures/`, etc. The repository likely has a `README.md` as well as other documentation. It may also include a CITATION file or license now that you’re thinking of sharing it.
*   **Dependency lists or environment files:** Files such as `requirements.txt`, `setup.py` (for Python packages), `environment.yml` for Conda, or `renv.lock` for R projects show up at this level. These files help specify what packages and versions are needed.
*   **Automation helpers:** You might have a `Makefile`, a `Snakefile`, or simply a shell script like `run_all.sh` or an R script that orchestrates the workflow. These encapsulate the series of steps to execute the entire analysis. If someone runs the right command (e.g. `make all` or `bash run_all.sh`), they’ll get the outputs without manual intervention.
*   **Data documentation:** Possibly a data dictionary, or at least a description of each data file and where it came from, perhaps in the README or a separate `data_description.md`. If using databases or external data sources, you have those access details recorded.
*   **Better computational notebooks (if used):** If you use Jupyter or R Markdown, you’re likely using them more effectively now – maybe breaking the work into multiple notebooks (one per analysis component) rather than one giant notebook, and using features like notebooks as scripts or parameterised notebooks to reduce manual tweaks.
*   **Issue tracker and project management (optional):** Some Level 3 projects start using tools like GitHub Issues or Trello to track to-dos, especially if multiple people are involved. While not strictly about computational reproducibility, these tools reflect a more project-minded approach that often goes hand-in-hand with Level 3.

## What works well at this level

*   **Solid reproducibility for you and your team:** At Level 3, you can confidently reproduce your own results whenever needed. If someone asks you to show how a figure was generated, you can typically run a script or two and regenerate it. Likewise, if a colleague wants to build on your work, you can point them to the repo, help them get the environment set up, and they’ll be up and running in short order. The fear of “oh no, what if my results are stuck in some spreadsheet on an old laptop” is largely gone.
*   **Efficient collaboration and sharing:** Because of the structured approach, it’s much easier for others to understand and use your work. **Onboarding a collaborator** is smoother – you can share the Git repository and they’ll see the code, data structure, and instructions. This fosters a culture of code sharing and transparency in your team or with external partners. It’s also easier to give or receive feedback on specific parts of the analysis (e.g., via code review or simply because others can read your code and suggest changes).
*   **Manageable maintenance:** The project is not overly complex to maintain. Since you haven’t gone full “over-engineered,” the amount of tooling is reasonable. When something breaks (say a package update introduces a bug), it’s not too hard to fix because you understand all the moving parts. The balance of rigour vs complexity is well-tuned; you’re reaping most benefits of reproducibility practices without drowning in dev-ops.
*   **Quicker iteration and adaptation:** Need to adapt the analysis for new data or a slightly different question? Level 3 makes that relatively quick. Because steps are encoded in scripts and you’re using version control, you can tweak parameters or code, re-run, and get updated results with far less work than at Level 1 or 2. If a reviewer asks for an extra analysis, you likely can incorporate it by adding a new script or extending your pipeline, and integrate it into the existing workflow smoothly.
*   **Confidence in results:** Knowing that your results are reproducible (at least in your environment) gives peace of mind. You’re less worried about accidental errors because if something seems off, you can rerun from scratch. The consistency of outcomes across runs (assuming no one has changed the code unexpectedly) means you trust that your results aren’t flukes of some transient manual mistake. It’s easier to debug or validate findings as well – you can rerun intermediate steps, add printouts or assertions in code, etc., without unraveling the whole analysis.

## What can go wrong (risks and limitations)

*   **Environment differences can still bite:** While you’ve captured environment info, you might still hit issues when moving to a **different platform or updating software**. For example, your pipeline might assume a Linux environment and a colleague tries it on Windows, leading to path issues or incompatible dependencies. Or a new version of a library comes out that isn’t captured in your `requirements.txt`, causing subtle differences. Without containers (that’s Level 4 territory), there’s some residual “works on my machine” risk if machines differ significantly.
*   **Some assembly required:** Reproducing your work at Level 3 might involve several setup steps: installing dependencies, obtaining or linking data, running the right sequence of scripts in order, etc. It’s doable (thanks to your documentation), but it’s not a **one-click process** yet. There’s room for human error in following the instructions (e.g., someone might forget to perform a setup step, or use the wrong version of a tool). Until you further automate those steps, reproducing the project demands a bit of care and understanding from the user.
*   **Limited automatic validation:** While your workflow runs, it might not have many built-in checks to tell you if something went wrong. For instance, if an intermediate file is empty because a step failed, the pipeline might still run to completion and produce an output (which could be flawed). Unless you manually inspect outputs or you’ve started adding some sanity-checks, issues can slip by. Continuous Integration or testing isn’t fully in place, so mistakes might be caught later in the research cycle (like during manuscript writing or peer review) rather than immediately.
*   **Scaling and reuse challenges:** The process at Level 3 is tailored to your project and perhaps your team. If someone outside your group wants to reuse your code or apply your pipeline to a slightly different problem, they may need to put in some effort to generalise or configure it. For example, paths might be hard-coded, or the code might assume a certain data format that others don’t use. In other words, the *portability* of your work is improved from earlier levels but not universal. Level 4 will tackle this by introducing more abstraction and portability (like containerising everything and parameterising workflows).
*   **Possibly growing complexity:** As projects live longer at Level 3, they can accumulate a lot of moving parts (multiple scripts, data files, etc.). Without careful management, you might experience some “sprawl” – e.g., old scripts that are no longer used but still sit in the repo, or configuration that is only in your head but not fully documented. The more people and features involved, the more you need to impose order (with clear guidelines or more advanced tools). If this isn’t addressed, there’s a risk of slipping back into a quasi-Level 2 state within a Level 3 project – basically organised on paper, but chaotic in practice. Regular refactoring and cleanup are needed to keep the sweet spot sweet.

## How to know if you’re at Level 3

Level 3 is a common state for research projects that have prioritised reproducibility and collaboration. Here are tell-tale signs that you’re working at the **Sweet Spot**:

*   **You have a public or private repository, and you use it regularly.** Your project’s history is in Git. You commit changes with messages like “Refactor data import function” rather than leaving all work in an unsaved state or only on a local disk. Colleagues might be listed as contributors, or at least you’ve shared the repository for them to view or use.
*   **Re-running isn’t scary anymore:** If someone asked for a new result or to tweak something, you know you can do it by adjusting parameters or code and running the pipeline, rather than manually re-doing analyses. You trust that things will run (though you might knock on wood for good measure). The thought of a complete rerun is a few hours of computation, not a few days of scrambling.
*   **Setup instructions exist and are followed:** When you set up the project on a new machine (or a new lab member does), you follow a written set of steps (install this software, run that script to download data, etc.). If those steps are done, the analysis runs end-to-end. If you find a new dependency during setup, you add it to the documentation so it won’t trip up the next person.
*   **Colleagues can reproduce your results (with minor help):** Perhaps you’ve tested the process by having a colleague follow your README independently. They might have asked a couple of questions (“I’m getting an error on step 3, what did I miss?”), which led you to improve the instructions. In the end, they were able to see the same output you did. If your PI or a collaborator can get the code running up to producing key figures, that’s a strong indicator of Level 3 success.
*   **You’re solving problems with code and tools, not manual labour:** When faced with a new analysis task, your instinct now is to write a script or find a tool, rather than doing it by hand. For example, instead of manually renaming 100 files, you’d write a small script to do it. This mindset means your future analyses will also be more reproducible (and it’s a sign you’re probably ready for even more advanced techniques).

If these points resonate with how you work, congratulations – **you’re in the Sweet Spot of reproducibility!** This is a level where your workflow is productive and others in your immediate circle can benefit from it. Many scientists find that this level gives most of the benefits of reproducible research without requiring too much additional effort or specialised knowledge. In fact, journals and reviewers are often satisfied with this level of reproducibility, especially if code and data are shared.

## Moving beyond Level 3

For many projects, staying at Level 3 may be perfectly sufficient. However, if you aim to share your work with a broader audience, support a highly diverse set of computers or users, or just want to **bulletproof your reproducibility against almost any scenario**, you might consider venturing into Level 4 (Assembly Line). Here are some steps to gently start that transition:

*   **Embrace containerisation:** Tools like **Docker** or **Singularity** allow you to package your entire computing environment (OS, libraries, everything) into one file. Experiment with creating a Docker image for your project. It can be as simple as writing a Dockerfile that says “start from python:3.10-slim, install these packages, then run the analysis.” You don’t need to force everyone to use it yet, but having it as an option means you can ensure the code runs in the same environment anywhere.
*   **Up your automation game with workflows:** If your pipeline is getting complex or you’re re-running it often, consider using a workflow manager like **Snakemake**, **Nextflow**, or similar (especially if you’re in a field that has common tools). These can take your scripts and turn them into a more formal pipeline that automatically handles dependencies between steps, parallel execution, and easy re-running of only what changed. It’s a bit of a learning curve, but you can start simple – maybe convert your sequential scripts into a handful of Snakemake rules, or use a CI tool to orchestrate running your existing scripts in order.
*   **Add continuous integration (CI) for basic checks:** Setting up a CI service (like GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, etc.) can automatically run your analysis (or a subset of it) whenever you make changes, ensuring everything still works. At Level 3, a light version might be: run a small test dataset through the pipeline to see that it finishes without errors. This guards against breaking things unknowingly. You could also configure CI to deploy your documentation or to run code style checks. Starting with one or two simple CI workflows will pave the way for the more extensive testing and automation seen at Level 4.
*   **Improve and standardise testing:** If your analysis involves custom code (functions, scripts), think about writing a few **basic tests**. These might be as simple as checking that a function returns expected output for a known input, or that running the entire pipeline yields a result file with the right properties (e.g., correct number of rows). You could integrate these into your CI pipeline so they run automatically. Testing might seem like a purely software-engineering thing, but in research it can catch mistakes (like a change that accidentally alters a statistical method’s parameters, etc.) before they affect your conclusions.
*   **Plan for longevity and hand-off:** Document not just the “how” of running the analysis, but the “why” behind key choices. At Level 4 and 5, the assumption is that your work could be handed to strangers (or your future self after a long gap) and still make sense. To prep for that, start adding rationale in your documentation: e.g., why you chose a certain threshold, or what to do if the data is updated. You can also look into creating a more narrative style of documentation (like a short **“getting started” guide** or using a documentation generator to publish a mini site for your project). This will make your project more approachable to newcomers, which is crucial as you move to higher levels of openness and collaboration.

By taking these steps, you’ll be edging into Level 4 territory, where reproducibility is even more *portable and automatic*. But even if you stay at Level 3, you’ve achieved a highly commendable state of reproducibility that will serve you and your team well. You’ve balanced rigour with practicality – the very definition of the Sweet Spot. Enjoy it, and remember that moving further is always an option, not a mandate, depending on the needs of your project. Onward and upward, or should we say, *pipeline and onward* – the choice is yours!
